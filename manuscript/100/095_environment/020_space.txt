### Bridging spaces 








In the end, we generally know - perhaps the optimists anyway - that the ideas behind the solutions brought to our spaces and lives comes from people that are living experiences first-hand, living the pains and needs, and valuing life and living aspirations, people that are optimistic to dreams for a better life, a better future, for themselves their friends and the civilization. A supporting point to that can be celebrated if we consider a phenomenon known as *User Innovation* [^footenote_ericvonhippel_userinnovation] that explains that users innovate and that many inventions like basketball and Krav Maga or Braille are just iconic examples of a pattern which explains that user innovation is everywhere and that innovation comes first from real people and not from producers. 

[^footenote_ericvonhippel_userinnovation]: MIT professor Eric von Hippel, through the credible book *Democratizing Innovation*, help us to extend further an understanding that the person who lives the experiences, the needs, are often the source of innovative solutions. The work recognizes characteristics of the phenomenon where users aren't passive recipients of products and services but active agents in their creation and refinement.

On the other hand, it is the entrepreneurial role that generally takes the credit for getting things done - at least up to this point since execution matters much more than ideas given that we don't have miraculous ways to execute ideas. Therefore, it goes along that they are in the position of being thankful, or frustrated, as they are caught between two spaces. On one hand, a source space, where they take things from, like resources, the how-to things. On the other hand, the target space, where their put their creations to test and where they put their hopes. That view, although simplistic, give us an opportunity to consider the challenges of being at the center - as a bridge - and in the position to solve the challenges presented by the entanglements with the whole environment.











To illustrate the challenges of that unique position, we shall go back to not-so-far, back-in-time, and consider the challenges of some of the doers that actually had put a dent in the universe - specifically redefining the the hail-riding experience which caused an impact to the taxi industry. The advent of the iPhone in 2007 brought to some builders a good news, first the recognition that people were actually buying that three-in-one device - an internet communicator system, a phone, and a music player [^footnote_stevejobs_threeinone] - as promoted by Steve Jobs. By 2008, they entrepreneurs were likely experiencing too other incremental development to the iPhone: an extended battery lifetime, support for the newly released 3G network which meant higher speed and better connectivity to the internet, and not to forget that the iPhone alread had a GPS support which meant for them the opportunity to make solutions that could track the location of people.

These meant or them an exciting space of the building blocks for making solutions for people's phones. In other words, a source space. With the motivation that sourcing was possible, they logically turned their attention to the target space, the place where people were living their lives, which prompted them to wonder about the experience of taking rides in the major cities around the world. Logically, their intention had to do with improving the experience from an existing experience, logically at the time framed by, or defined, by existing incremental developments generally observed as characteristics of an experience provided by the taxi industry. As an example, if one wanted a ride, they could "get a cab," by making a phone call to a central. Or, when in the streets in the busy centers such as of New York, they could wave their hands to a yellow car. Or, they could walk to a specific taxi area. After entering the cab, they were in the experience as it were. I remember the day when I left my phone in a cab in San Franscisco and how comfortable I was to know that I would never see it again. In another experienced, when I were a host to my friends from Mozilla organization visiting Brazil, I caught myself taking a ride in the streets of Porto Alegre at 110 km/h afraid to ask the driver to reduce the speed while holding my hands tight and fearing a big news for the next day - The end of Mozilla project from a crash in Brazil. 

These were some elements that prompted some entrepreneurs to become that bridge and to connect these different spaces. Which took them to ask other questions: Are people really buying that expensive phone? Would it be possible to develop a solution where they could order a ride from any place by tapping the screen on their phone? Would they be able to monitor the driver approaching in the screen and how how long would it take to pick up? Would it be possible to follow the movement looking a map in real-time? And perhaps to see the fares upfront, ahead of time, before accepting the ride; or to pick the size of the car, and a lot more such as to be able to rate the driver behavior and safety aspects. The more they asked the more the bridge seemed real which took them to also ask a critical quesetion - if they were to make a solution, how easy/difficult would be for people to install that and tell others too. 

Thankfully, Apple had created a place enabling people to browse and install these solutions (apps) which were essentially a marketplace [^footnote_apple_marketplace] integrated in the phone. Basically, anyone using an iPhone could browse applications made by different companies where they could read more about who were the company behind, and able to install, and to use it. For developers, that meant a green light again, asking them to accelerate towards disrupting the old hail-riding experience, or the cab industry, or taxi industry, you name it. 

[^footnote_apple_marketplace]: In 2008, Apple Inc. launched its "App Store" which consisted of being a digital marketplace bundled with the iPhones and enabling people to download applications, apps, fundamentally changing the way software is discovered, installed which market implications in terms of distribution and monetization.

On other hands, of other founders, they thought that in order for riders to have a better experience, the known situation of the hail-hailing experience needed to change. If we look at the original business presentation from Uber - Uber's original pitch deck (2017, August 23) - they point to the poor experience of ride taking as connected with the taxi monopolies.





Now that history shows that Uber survived, and given that many riders don't even know the world as it were before, can be challenging for us to see how these founders were struggling at the time. [^foonote_bundling_old]: A historical perspective about the usage of "bundling" from the past and the modern usage is given by in Bundling (2000) which elaborates about the original usage from an old courtship practice where young couples spent nights together in a single bed, either tied or "bundled" up to prevent physical intimacies; to the usage in modern business terminology where "bundling" referred to the strategy of selling multiple products or services together as a single package, offering convenience but also raising concerns about potential anti-competitive effects.
